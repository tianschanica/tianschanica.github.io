The Great Game of Science

Central Asia — the last unknown continent of the 19th century

In the mid-19th century, the heart of Asia remained a blank on European maps. The Tian Shan — "Heavenly Mountains" — rising between the Russian Empire and China, had never been systematically explored by science. Alexander von Humboldt, the era's greatest geographer, theorized they were volcanic. He was wrong.

Two Russian scientists would change everything. Pyotr Semyonov crossed the Tian Shan in 1856–57, proving the mountains were glacial, not volcanic, and mapping their peaks for the first time. Then Nikolai Przhevalsky, a military officer turned geographer, conducted five major expeditions across the wider Central Asian expanse — Mongolia, Tibet, the Gobi, the Tarim Basin — between 1870 and his death in 1888.

Together, their work filled in the blank at the center of the world's largest continent, producing the first accurate maps of regions that had been crossed by Silk Road caravans but never measured, described, or understood by modern science.

Pyotr Semyonov

Tyan-Shansky · 1827 – 1914 · Geographer & Statesman

П. П. Семёнов-Тян-Шанский

Born into a noble family in Ryazan Oblast in 1827, Pyotr Semyonov was already an accomplished botanist and geographer when he first read Alexander von Humboldt's monumental work Asie Centrale in 1849. Humboldt's confident assertion that the Tian Shan peaks were volcanic struck Semyonov as unproven — and he resolved to test it himself.

After two years of preparation in Berlin under Ritter and Humboldt himself, Semyonov departed in 1856 for Verny (present-day Almaty). He was the first European scientist to cross the Terskey Alatau range and descend into the Central Tian Shan — territory no Western geographer had ever described.

His two seasons of fieldwork (1856 and 1857) produced the first accurate survey of the Tian Shan, its glacial hydrology, its flora and fauna zones, and the approximate heights of its major peaks. He concluded definitively: the mountains were not volcanic. In 1906, Tsar Nicholas II added "Tyan-Shansky" to his surname in recognition of the life's work.

Expedition Journals

1856
First Expedition — The Terskey Crossing
Verny → Issyk-Kul → Terskey Alatau → Naryn · approx. 2,000 km
May 1856
Departure from St. Petersburg. Semyonov travels via Siberia to Barnaul, collecting botanical specimens en route.
Jul 1856
Arrives at Verny (Almaty). First views of the Zailiysky Alatau range above the city. Begins organizing the mountain expedition.
Aug 1856
First crossing of the Zailiysky Alatau. Descends toward the Chu River. First approach to the western shore of Issyk-Kul — the great alpine lake at 1,607 m that had barely been surveyed.
Sep 1856
Terskey Alatau crossing. Semyonov becomes the first European to cross this range south of Issyk-Kul into the Upper Naryn basin. Discovers the glacial headwaters of the Naryn River (the principal source of the Syr Darya).
Oct 1856
Return to Verny. No volcanic rock anywhere. The foundation of Humboldt's volcanic theory begins to crack. Semyonov plans a second, deeper expedition.
Key result First scientific survey of eastern Issyk-Kul and the Terskey Alatau range. Identification of Naryn River headwaters.
1857
Second Expedition — Into the Central Tian Shan
Verny → Saryjaz Valley → Petrov Glacier · approx. 2,500 km
Jun 1857
Second departure from Verny. Semyonov heads east along the southern shore of Issyk-Kul with a larger scientific party including geologists and botanists.
Jul 1857
Karakol area. Documents the eastern end of Issyk-Kul and begins ascent through the Terskey Alatau via new passes, entering the Saryjaz valley.
Aug 5, 1857
Petrov Glacier. Semyonov reaches the Petrov Glacier at approximately 4,000 m — one of the major glaciers of the Central Tian Shan. From here, the great peaks of the Khan Tengri massif are visible for the first time to a European scientist.
Aug 18, 1857
Definitive refutation of Humboldt's theory. Extensive geological sampling across the Tian Shan reveals no volcanic rock whatsoever. The mountains are crystalline and metamorphic. Semyonov estimates peak heights — Khan Tengri is identified as approximately 7,000 m.
Oct 1857
Return to Russia. Semyonov brings back 300 plant species (87 new to science), 1,000 insect specimens, extensive rock collections, and the first accurate map of the Central Tian Shan.
Key result Tian Shan confirmed as non-volcanic. First sighting of Khan Tengri massif by a European scientist. 87 new plant species described.

Nikolai Przhevalsky

1839 – 1888 · Colonel & Geographer · 5 Expeditions

Н. М. Пржевальский

Nikolai Przhevalsky was born in 1839 in the Smolensk gubernia and commissioned as an officer in the Russian Imperial Army. Yet from his earliest years, geography was his true vocation. He taught geography at the Warsaw Junker School, writing his first geographical work on the Ussuri region before anyone had heard of Central Asia in connection with his name.

Between 1870 and 1885 he completed four monumental expeditions across Central Asia — into Mongolia, Lop Nor, Tibet, and the Gobi — covering approximately 40,000 km on foot and horseback through unmapped terrain. He was the first European to describe Lop Nor (the wandering lake), to survey northeastern Tibet, and to cross the Qaidam Basin.

He discovered and described dozens of new species, including the last truly wild horse on Earth — Equus przewalskii, Przhevalsky's horse — and the wild Bactrian camel. He died at Karakol on the shores of Issyk-Kul on November 1, 1888, at the very outset of his fifth expedition. The town was renamed Przhevalsk in his honor (today Karakol, Kyrgyzstan).

Expedition Journals

I
Mongolia and Northern China
Kyakhta → Mongolia → Qinghai Lake → return · 11,800 km · Nov 1870 – Sep 1873
Nov 1870
Departure from Kyakhta (Russia–Mongolia border). Przhevalsky leads a party of 3 men, 2 Cossacks, crossing into Mongolia toward Urga (Ulaanbaatar).
1871
Inner Mongolia and the Gobi. Przhevalsky traverses the eastern Gobi Desert, crosses into Inner Mongolia and reaches Dolon Nor and the upper reaches of the Yellow River. Extensive ornithological and botanical collection.
1872
Ala Shan and Ordos. Crosses the Ala Shan range (Helan Mountains), enters the great bend of the Yellow River. Collects 100 bird species new to science.
1872
Qinghai Lake (Kuku Nor). Przhevalsky becomes the first Russian scientist to properly survey and describe Qinghai Lake — at 4,500 km² the largest saltwater lake in China and the largest lake in Central Asia. He records its dimensions, depth estimates, and surrounding steppe fauna.
Sep 1873
Return to Kyakhta. 11,800 km covered in 2 years 10 months. The expedition publishes "Mongolia and the Land of the Tanguts" (1875). Przhevalsky is awarded the Imperial Russian Geographical Society's highest honor.
Key results First scientific survey of Qinghai Lake. Extensive Gobi Desert natural history. 100+ new bird species. Botanical collections from inner Mongolia.
II
The Lop Nor Expedition
Kuldzha → Tian Shan → Lop Nor → return · 4,190 km · Aug 1876 – Oct 1877
Aug 1876
Departure from Kuldzha (Yining), in the Ili Valley of present-day China. Przhevalsky crosses the eastern Tian Shan range, descending into the Turpan depression — one of the lowest and hottest points in Asia.
Jan 1877
Discovery of Lop Nor. Przhevalsky reaches and surveys Lop Nor — a shallow salt lake in the Tarim Basin that had been known from Chinese records but never properly mapped by Western science. He establishes it is a wandering lake, shifting position over centuries. The discovery triggers decades of cartographic debate.
Feb 1877
Dunhuang region. Przhevalsky reaches the Dunhuang oasis area and the Qilian Shan foothills, adding the eastern Tarim Basin to the map.
Oct 1877
Return via Tian Shan. Przhevalsky recrosses the Tian Shan. Publishes "From Kuldzha Across the Tian Shan to Lop Nor" (1878). First discovery of wild Bactrian camels living around Lop Nor.
Key results First scientific survey of Lop Nor. Discovery of wild Bactrian camel (Camelus ferus). First crossing and mapping of eastern Tian Shan.
III
First Tibetan Expedition
Zaisan → Hami → Lop Nor → Tibet (turned ~250 km from Lhasa) · ~8,000 km · Mar 1879 – Oct 1880
Mar 1879
Departure from Zaisan (eastern Kazakhstan). Przhevalsky aims south toward Tibet with 12 men, aiming to reach Lhasa — a goal that had fired the imagination of Western exploration for decades.
Summer 1879
Across the Tarim Basin. Via Hami and Lop Nor, the expedition crosses into the Qaidam (Tsaidam) Basin — a high-altitude depression at 2,700 m filled with salt marshes. First scientific survey of this region.
Aug 1879
Discovery of Przhevalsky's Horse. Near Lop Nor, specimens of a truly wild horse are obtained — unknown to Western science. Named Equus przewalskii. The last wild horse on Earth, it survives today only through breeding programs in zoos and reserves.
Jan 1880
Blocked 250 km from Lhasa. A Tibetan delegation halts the expedition near the town of Nagchu (Naqu). The Tibetan authorities refuse passage to Lhasa. Przhevalsky turns north, having mapped the northern Tibetan plateau to approximately 33°N latitude.
Oct 1880
Return to Russia. Przhevalsky publishes "From Zaisan through Hami to Tibet" (1883). The first scientific account of the northern Tibetan plateau, Qaidam Basin, and Qilian Shan.
Key results Discovery of Equus przewalskii (Przhevalsky's horse). First survey of northern Tibet and Qaidam Basin. Blocked 250 km from Lhasa.
IV
Second Tibetan Expedition
Kyakhta → Mongolia → Tsaidam → Tibet → Keriya → Lop Nor → return · ~7,800 km · Oct 1883 – Nov 1885
Oct 1883
Departure from Kyakhta. Przhevalsky crosses Mongolia to the Gobi Desert, following a more westerly route than his first expedition into the heart of the continent.
1884
Tsaidam and Tibet. The expedition makes a deep penetration of the Tibetan Plateau, reaching approximately 34°N — further south than any previous expedition. The Yellow River headwaters are surveyed and mapped for the first time.
1884
Keriya (Yutian) and the Kunlun Shan. Przhevalsky explores the Keriya Darya river valley south of the Tarim, mapping the northern faces of the Kunlun range — the great barrier between Tibet and Central Asia.
1885
Return via Lop Nor. Przhevalsky revisits Lop Nor and surveys its position again — confirming its wandering nature. Full publication follows: "From Kyakhta to the Headwaters of the Yellow River" (1888).
Key results Survey of Yellow River headwaters. Deep penetration of Tibetan Plateau. Mapping of Keriya Darya and Kunlun Shan. Confirmation of Lop Nor's wandering position.
V
Third Tibetan Expedition — Unfinished
Karakol, Issyk-Kul · Sep – Nov 1, 1888
Sep 1888
Departure for Tashkent. Przhevalsky sets out for what would be his fifth Central Asian expedition, aiming once more to attempt the crossing to Lhasa. He travels via Samarkand and Tashkent toward Issyk-Kul.
Oct 1888
Arrives at Karakol. On the eastern shore of Issyk-Kul, where Semyonov had explored 30 years before, Przhevalsky falls ill — almost certainly typhoid fever, contracted from drinking Chu River water during a hunting trip.
Nov 1, 1888
Death at Karakol. Przhevalsky dies aged 49, requesting to be buried on the shore of Issyk-Kul, "in his explorer's clothes." He is buried on a promontory above the lake. The town of Karakol is renamed Przhevalsk by Imperial decree.
Epitaph "The first condition of success in the career of an explorer is good health, the second is perseverance, the third patience, and the fourth modesty." — Przhevalsky

The Catalogue of Discovery

What the expeditions brought back to science

Semyonov
🏔
Non-volcanic Tian Shan

Refuted Humboldt's volcanic theory. The Tian Shan are crystalline and metamorphic in origin — entirely glacial in their modern form. This was one of the most important geological corrections of 19th-century geography.

Semyonov
🌊
Source of the Naryn River

Identified the glacial headwaters of the Naryn, the principal tributary of the Syr Darya — one of Central Asia's two great rivers. Documented how the Tian Shan glaciers sustain river flow to the Aral Sea basin.

Semyonov
🌿
87 New Plant Species

Over 300 botanical specimens were returned, 87 of which were new to science. The Tian Shan flora showed a unique transitional character between Mediterranean, Siberian, and Chinese floral zones.

Przhevalsky
🐎
Przhevalsky's Horse

Equus przewalskii — the last true wild horse on Earth, distinct from all domesticated breeds. Specimens collected near Lop Nor in 1879. Today the species survives only through captive breeding programs, with reintroduction underway in Mongolia and China.

Przhevalsky
🐪
Wild Bactrian Camel

Camelus ferus — confirmed as genetically distinct from the domestic Bactrian camel. Found around the Lop Nor basin. Now Critically Endangered, with fewer than 1,000 remaining in the wild in the Gobi and Gashun Gobi.

Przhevalsky
🏞
Lop Nor — The Wandering Lake

The shallow salt lake in the Tarim Basin, known from ancient Chinese records, was first properly surveyed and mapped by Przhevalsky in 1877. He established its wandering nature — the lake shifts position by centuries due to river course changes. Lop Nor dried up entirely by the 1970s.

Przhevalsky
🗺
Northern Tibet Mapped

Przhevalsky produced the first European survey of the northern Tibetan Plateau, the Qaidam (Tsaidam) Basin, the Qilian Shan, and the headwaters of the Yellow River. He reached approximately 33–34°N latitude — 250 km from Lhasa at his closest approach.

Przhevalsky
🦅
218 Species Described

Over four expeditions, Przhevalsky described and collected specimens of 218 species new to Western science — mammals, birds, reptiles, fish, and invertebrates. The Siberian white crane, Przewalski's gazelle, and numerous rodent species bear connections to his collections.

Legacy

How their work shaped the map of Central Asia

01

The Cartographic Revolution

Before these expeditions, the interior of Central Asia was represented on European maps by a blank or by ancient Arab and Chinese descriptions redrawn by guesswork. By 1890, the Tian Shan, Kunlun, northern Tibet, the Tarim Basin, the Gobi Desert, and Mongolia were mapped with reasonable accuracy for the first time.

02

The Geographical Society

Both explorers were central figures in the Imperial Russian Geographical Society. Their published accounts — richly detailed with maps, natural history illustrations, and ethnographic observations — inspired a generation of Russian scholars to follow. Kozlov, Roborovsky, and Potanin all continued the work.

03

Species Conservation

Przhevalsky's horse was brought back from extinction purely because specimens were collected and described in 1879. Every living Przewalski's horse today descends from 12–15 individuals held in zoos after the wild population vanished around 1969. Reintroduction programs in Mongolia and China have established wild herds numbering around 2,000.

04

Named in Their Honor

Semyonov-Tyan-Shansky lends his name to a mountain range in Kyrgyzstan, a glacier, and several dozen plant and insect species. Przhevalsky's name is borne by the city of Karakol (briefly Przhevalsk), the horse, a camel subspecies, a gazelle, a finch, and numerous species of plants and invertebrates.
